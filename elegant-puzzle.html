Chapter 2: Organizations

2.1 Sizing Teams

  • Manager-to-Engineer Ratio: Managers should typically support 6-8 engineers.
  • Manager-of-Managers Ratio: Managers of managers should support 4-6 managers.

2.2.2 Dealing with Stakeholder Impatience

  • When a team is repaying technical debt, stakeholders often become impatient for new feature delivery.
  • Obligation: Prevent this impatience from causing a backslide into accumulating more debt.

2.3.1 Team-First Mentality

  • High-Performing Teams: Sustained productivity comes from high-performing teams. Disassembling them significantly reduces productivity, even if individual members are retained.
  • Value of Stability: High-performing teams are sacred and take time to gel. Shifting individuals across teams can reset this process, particularly when team cultures differ significantly.

2.3.3 The Cost of Adding Resources

  • Counterintuitive Slowdowns: Adding resources to a team can often slow it down due to the creation of new upstream constraints.

2.3.4 Scope Shifts and Rotations

  • Shifting Scope: Shifting project scope is generally preferable to moving people, as it avoids the costs of re-gelling and preserves existing system behavior.
  • Temporary Rotations: Rotating individuals for a fixed period into areas needing help can be effective. This allows them to retain their primary team identity while providing focused support.

2.4.3 Managing Entropy Through System Design

  • Flexible Software Design: Design software to be flexible from the start. This is described as "fail open and layer policy."
  • Avoid Rewrites: The best system rewrite is the one that doesn't happen. Avoid baking in arbitrary policy decisions that are likely to change.
  • Generic Interfaces: Keep interfaces generic to avoid complex migration phases during system updates. This allows for faster iteration and reduces the need to maintain multiple versions. While there's a cost to maintaining an extra layer of abstraction, it can save time and effort in the long run, especially after multiple rewrites.
  • Gatekeeping: Using humans for gatekeeping can create negative social dynamics and is inefficient. Treat gatekeeping as a bug, not a feature. This appears to be more prevalent in cultures without a strong hierarchical structure, such as those with mandatory military service.

Organizational Risk

  • Organizational Debt: Similar to technical debt, organizational debt arises from compromises made to "just get it done." It involves people and cultural issues.
  • Managing Organizational Debt:
    • Identify a few areas of organizational debt to actively improve.
    • Ensure you're making progress in these areas.
    • Give yourself permission to do other areas poorly.
    • Develop an explicit plan with your manager and agree on what constitutes reasonable progress.
    • These issues are still stored with your other bags of risk and responsibility, but you've agreed on expectations.

2.6.1 Defining Your Role

  • Document Your Role: In meetings, write down your role to clarify responsibilities and expectations.
